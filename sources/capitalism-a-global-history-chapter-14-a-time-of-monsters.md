# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 14: A Time of Monsters: Industrial Capitalism, 1918-1945

## Summary

Chapter 14 treats the interwar period and World War II as a crisis of industrial capitalism. Beckert connects labor militancy, depression, fascism, nationalism, colonial unrest, state intervention, and wartime mobilization to a global search for new ways to manage capitalism.

## Key Claims

- Industrial capitalism after 1918 faced intense instability from war, inflation, labor conflict, depression, and imperial crisis.
- Fascist, socialist, liberal, colonial, and nationalist projects all tried to answer the failures of the prewar order.
- The crisis strengthened demands for economic sovereignty in colonized and semi-colonized regions.
- World War II and its aftermath accelerated decolonizing visions of industrial development.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Senegalese railway workers demanding better wages and conditions in 1919.
- Links colonial labor conflict to broader crises in industrial capitalism.
- Ends by pointing toward a "colored" or postcolonial capitalism that would reshape the twentieth century.
