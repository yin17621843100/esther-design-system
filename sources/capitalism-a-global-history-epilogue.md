# Capitalism: A Global History - Epilogue: The Possibilities of an Island and the Future of Capitalism

## Summary

The epilogue returns to capitalism's future through contemporary Cambodian garment work and the Channel Island of Brecqhou. Beckert uses these examples to show capitalism's continuing power to reorganize labor, mobility, sovereignty, geography, and imagination while leaving its future politically open.

## Key Claims

- Contemporary global capitalism still creates rapid proletarianization, especially for women workers in export manufacturing.
- Global production chains connect rural villages, factory labor, infrastructure, foreign capital, and consumer markets.
- Capital can also "secede" into legal and territorial niches that protect wealth and autonomy.
- The book closes by asking future historians to see capitalism as a strange historical civilization rather than a natural order.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Cambodian garment workers commuting to factories near Phnom Penh.
- Follows Srey Mau's movement between village life, factory work, migration, debt, layoffs, and wage dependence.
- Ends by imagining future historians trying to understand capitalism as a puzzling past civilization.
