# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 5: Intensifying Industry, 1600-1750

## Summary

Chapter 5 examines the intensification of manufacturing before mechanized industrial capitalism. Beckert highlights rural and proto-industrial production, including textile regions, to show how capital escaped urban regulation and reorganized household and craft labor for distant markets.

## Key Claims

- Manufacturing intensified before the Industrial Revolution, often outside cities and guild controls.
- Capital's mobility allowed it to move between trade, agriculture, and production in ways older property forms could not.
- Rural manufacturing created new links among merchants, households, artisans, raw materials, and global demand.
- This intensification revealed capitalism's revolutionary potential but did not yet produce self-sustaining industrial transformation.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Jelenia Gora as a textile-producing center embedded in wider linen markets.
- Treats Indian weavers and European rural industries as part of a broader global manufacturing story.
- Ends by setting up the "perfect storm" that would make mechanized industrial capitalism possible.
