# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 8: Capturing the Hinterland, 1780-1860

## Summary

Chapter 8 argues that industrial capitalism depended on the capture and reorganization of commodity hinterlands. Beckert links industrial centers to enslaved labor, plantation zones, rural producers, transport networks, and unequal institutions that supplied raw materials and food.

## Key Claims

- Industrial growth required massive hinterlands for cotton, food, coffee, sugar, minerals, and other commodities.
- Coercive and unequal labor systems were not external to industrial capitalism; they enabled it.
- The Haitian Revolution and Toussaint Louverture show that enslaved and colonized people actively shaped capitalism's trajectory.
- The "inclusive" institutions of industrial centers depended on extractive institutions elsewhere.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Toussaint Louverture and the revolutionary crisis in slave plantation capitalism.
- Connects commodity frontiers to industrial urban centers through trade, finance, and state power.
- Ends by describing connected diversity as the basis of an emerging capitalist civilization.
