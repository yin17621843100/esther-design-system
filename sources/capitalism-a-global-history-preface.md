# Capitalism: A Global History - Preface

## Summary

The preface frames [[capitalism]] as a world-historical force that shapes work, consumption, politics, landscapes, technology, and everyday thought. Beckert presents the book as a global history of a recent but pervasive human-made order, not as a natural or timeless condition.

## Key Claims

- Capitalism is difficult to escape because it structures both material life and common categories such as "the economy," value, time, and growth.
- The capitalist revolution is geographically uneven: some places have long histories of capitalist connection, while many rural and peripheral regions entered capitalist relations much later.
- The book will follow roughly one thousand years of capitalist development across multiple regions rather than begin from one nation, industry, or origin point.
- The purpose of the history is explanatory and diagnostic: understanding how capitalism became dominant may reveal the levers by which its future can be changed.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens from the premise that contemporary life is organized by capitalist accumulation and global market connection.
- Uses examples of everyday commodities and labor links to show how distant production enters ordinary life.
- Positions capitalism as both recent and deeply embedded, which sets up the book's historical method.
