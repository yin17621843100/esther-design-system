# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 13: Enclosures

## Summary

Chapter 13 places the modern state at the center of capitalism's late nineteenth-century reconstruction. Beckert broadens "enclosure" beyond land to include territorial, legal, imperial, national, social, and intellectual boundaries that stabilized and accelerated accumulation.

## Key Claims

- Capitalism's reconstruction depended on stronger states with greater capacity to define property, regulate labor, make war, and organize territory.
- Enclosure included not only fences and commons but also national markets, imperial territories, legal codes, class rights, and economic categories.
- State power became more deeply entangled with capital, even as different countries followed liberal, democratic, autocratic, or imperial paths.
- The tensions of territorialized capital, inequality, and imperial hierarchy fed into the crises of the early twentieth century.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Hokkaido and Ainu labor as a concrete example of territorial and imperial enclosure.
- Treats wars of state-building and imperial expansion as part of capitalism's institutional history.
- Ends by connecting reconstructed capitalism to the unresolved national, social, and imperial questions after World War I.
