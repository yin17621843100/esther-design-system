# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 17: Riding the Tiger: A Neoliberal Age, 1973-2008

## Summary

Chapter 17 presents the [[neoliberal-age]] as a reconstruction of capitalism after the postwar settlement. Beckert begins with Chile after the 1973 coup and follows market-oriented restructuring, labor weakening, financialization, privatization, global production, and the 2008 crisis.

## Key Claims

- Neoliberalism emerged as an attack on labor-centric Keynesianism and import-substitution development.
- Chile was an early and revealing case because market freedom was imposed through authoritarian violence.
- By the 1980s and 1990s, neoliberal policies became mainstream across much of the world.
- Financialized capitalism produced dense global interdependence, making the US real-estate crisis of 2008 a world crisis.
- The 2008 bailouts showed again that market capitalism depended on massive state action during crisis.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with US embassy reporting on Chilean austerity after the coup against Salvador Allende.
- Connects authoritarian political force, trained economists, austerity, and market-oriented reform.
- Ends with global state rescue efforts during the 2008 crisis.
