# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 6: The Perfect Storm

## Summary

Chapter 6 explains the convergence that prepared the way for industrial capitalism. Beckert presents industrial breakthrough as the product of multiple connected conditions: merchant capital, colonial resources, state power, global markets, transformed countryside production, and ideological shifts that naturalized market society.

## Key Claims

- Industrial capitalism emerged from a global root system rather than from one domestic cause.
- Capitalist development was both private and public: entrepreneurs and capital owners depended on states, imperial policy, violence, and infrastructure.
- Opposition from elites and commoners slowed capitalist transformation, making the breakthrough contingent.
- Adam Smith captured part of this moment but abstracted away power, violence, and the state.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Bordeaux and Atlantic commerce as examples of capital gathering around imperial trade.
- Shows that several nodes of the archipelago had to connect before industrial transformation could accelerate.
- Ends by framing Smith as a brilliant but partial interpreter of the world that made industrial capitalism possible.
