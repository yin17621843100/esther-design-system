# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 12: Reconstructing Labor, 1870-1920

## Summary

Chapter 12 argues that reconstructing labor was central to reconstructing capitalism. As capital owners expanded rural and urban production, they needed new systems for recruiting, disciplining, retaining, moving, and classifying workers across both industrial centers and commodity frontiers.

## Key Claims

- Capital accumulation required labor reconstruction as much as technological or financial innovation.
- New labor regimes included wage work, migrant labor, contract labor, colonial labor systems, household labor, and coercive forms of recruitment.
- The reconstruction of labor responded to worker resistance and to the declining legitimacy of older slavery-centered systems.
- By 1900, capitalism had become more industrial, consolidated, global, and dependent on increasingly managed labor relations.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens by foregrounding capital owners' need to mobilize men, women, and children across fields, factories, mines, docks, and households.
- Connects recruitment and control to global commodity demand.
- Ends by summarizing the new capitalism of consolidated firms, new industries, and reordered labor.
