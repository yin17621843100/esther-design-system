# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 2: Capitalists Without Capitalism

## Summary

Chapter 2 deepens the argument that capitalists existed before capitalism. Beckert surveys merchant networks across Asia, Africa, Europe, and the Americas to show how traders accumulated wealth and connected distant regions while most economic life remained governed by agrarian, tributary, household, and coercive systems.

## Key Claims

- Merchant communities created a distinct layer of economic life but did not yet remake society around capitalist accumulation.
- Similarities among far-flung traders came from shared pressures of distance, risk, capital mobility, and cross-cultural exchange.
- Wealthy merchants could influence states and culture, but they generally remained constrained by rulers, landed elites, religious authorities, and local institutions.
- There was no straight line from premodern merchant capital to modern capitalism; the later capitalist revolution was contingent.

## Evidence Anchors

- Uses examples of Gujarati, Swahili, Chinese, European, and Mesoamerican trading worlds.
- Emphasizes both long-distance connection and the thinness of capital's reach into everyday production.
- Ends by warning against treating early merchant networks as a guaranteed path to modern capitalism.
