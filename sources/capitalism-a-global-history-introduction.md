# Capitalism: A Global History - Introduction

## Summary

The introduction uses the 1639 Robert Keayne case in Massachusetts to show that profit-seeking market norms were historically contested rather than self-evident. Beckert defines the book's central problem: how a once-strange logic of buying cheaply, selling dearly, accumulating capital, and organizing life through markets became ordinary.

## Key Claims

- Capitalist behavior had to be socially legitimated; it was not always treated as morally or politically acceptable.
- Capitalism should be studied through the historical traces it leaves in institutions, landscapes, labor systems, and mental categories.
- The book begins with merchants because they embodied early practices of capital accumulation before capitalism became a dominant social order.
- The history must explain both capitalism's productive dynamism and its coercive, unequal, and destructive features.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Keayne's punishment for overcharging, a case that reveals early modern limits on market freedom.
- Moves from a local moral economy to a global history of capital accumulation.
- Ends by directing the narrative toward Aden around 1150 and the earliest merchant capitalists.
