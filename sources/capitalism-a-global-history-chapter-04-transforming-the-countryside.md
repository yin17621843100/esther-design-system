# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 4: Transforming the Countryside, 1550-1750

## Summary

Chapter 4 follows capital into the countryside, especially through plantation agriculture, imperial commodity production, and rural reorganization. Beckert shows that capitalism's growth depended not only on cities and merchants but also on violent transformations of land, labor, and agricultural production.

## Key Claims

- The early modern countryside became a major site of capitalist expansion.
- Plantations, especially in the Atlantic world, linked capital accumulation to slavery, coercion, ecological transformation, and imperial power.
- Capitalists pushed into rural production because cities and regulated craft systems often limited accumulation.
- The transformation of agriculture intensified global hierarchies, increasingly favoring Europe while relying on non-European land and labor.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Barbados and the Noell family as examples of merchant capital moving into plantation investment.
- Connects Atlantic plantations, Indian and African trade, and European demand into a single global process.
- Ends by transitioning from transformed agriculture to intensified manufacturing.
