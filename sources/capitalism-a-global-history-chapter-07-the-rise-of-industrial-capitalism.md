# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 7: The Rise of Industrial Capitalism, 1760-1850

## Summary

Chapter 7 treats the Industrial Revolution as capitalism's major acceleration rather than its origin. Mechanization, factories, fossil energy, and expanding markets turned earlier streams of capital into a torrent and reorganized production, labor, and global commodity flows.

## Key Claims

- Industrial capitalism grew out of earlier global connecting, rural transformation, manufacturing intensification, and state-building.
- The Industrial Revolution made economic change more self-sustaining and consequential than earlier capitalist expansions.
- Industrial capitalism coexisted and clashed with older forms such as slavery, aristocratic power, and old-regime political institutions.
- The new system's effects were felt strongly in the countryside, which supplied labor, food, and raw materials.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens by contrasting the previous slow spread of capitalist streams with the acceleration after 1760.
- Places industrial transformation inside a wider global and rural history.
- Ends by moving toward the hinterlands that industrial capitalism needed to capture.
