# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 16: Taming Industrial Capitalism, 1945-1973

## Summary

Chapter 16 describes the post-1945 "golden age" in the industrial heartlands, when growth, mass production, mass consumption, welfare institutions, labor power, state management, and Cold War pressure temporarily stabilized and legitimated industrial capitalism.

## Key Claims

- Industrial capitalism was partly tamed through welfare states, labor settlements, macroeconomic management, and rising mass consumption.
- The postwar settlement depended on technical change, cheap raw materials, powerful labor movements, and competition with communism.
- The era reduced some inequalities in the industrial heartlands while relying on global resource flows and rapidly increasing carbon emissions.
- By the 1970s, inflation, labor conflict, slowed growth, and political dissatisfaction signaled the end of the golden age.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with the "kitchen debate" as a symbolic contest over consumer capitalism.
- Uses the "thirty glorious years" and comparable national labels to mark a distinctive postwar period.
- Ends with the collapse of confidence in the postwar model by the late 1970s.
