# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 9: A Capitalist Civilization, 1830-1880

## Summary

Chapter 9 describes capitalism becoming a civilization: a broad material, cultural, institutional, and ideological order. Beckert shows how museums, consumption, urban life, bourgeois culture, and claims about economic laws helped naturalize capitalism while its social conflicts remained unresolved.

## Key Claims

- Capitalism moved beyond isolated nodes and became a wider civilization with its own institutions, values, and cultural forms.
- Capitalist elites worked to legitimize accumulation, market society, and the authority of economic laws.
- The civilization remained incomplete because many people rejected its inequalities, disruptions, and moral claims.
- Capitalist social relations produced fissures that would lead to waves of rebellion and reconstruction.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with New York's Metropolitan Museum of Art as a symbol of capitalist elite culture and civic display.
- Links culture and ideology to material transformations in production, consumption, and empire.
- Ends by describing capitalist civilization as dominant enough to generate major social and political crises.
