# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 1: Islands of Capital

## Summary

Chapter 1 argues that capitalism has no single birthplace. Instead, Beckert describes an "archipelago" of merchant communities and capital nodes, with Aden as an early example, where trade, credit, shipping, and commodity circulation created islands of capital inside wider noncapitalist worlds.

## Key Claims

- Capitalism began as a process rather than as an event in one city, nation, or industry.
- Early capitalists were primarily merchants who connected distant producers and consumers and accumulated capital through trade, finance, and logistics.
- These merchant nodes existed inside societies still largely organized around subsistence, tribute, household production, and coercive rule.
- The existence of merchant capital did not make capitalism inevitable; these islands supplied resources and practices that later became important.

## Evidence Anchors

- Aden functions as an early Indian Ocean trading hub linking Asia, Africa, Arabia, and Europe.
- Merchant communities show recurring institutions across great distances: shipping, credit, currency exchange, storage, risk management, and long-distance brokerage.
- The chapter closes by stressing both the growth of tradable surpluses and the limits that still kept capitalism from becoming a full social order.
