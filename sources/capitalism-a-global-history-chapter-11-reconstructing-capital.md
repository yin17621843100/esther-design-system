# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 11: Reconstructing Capital, 1870-1914

## Summary

Chapter 11 examines the late nineteenth-century reconstruction of capital through heavy industry, large firms, fixed investment, vertical and horizontal integration, protectionism, and intensified state involvement. Capital became larger, more organized, more nationalized in rhetoric, and still deeply global in practice.

## Key Claims

- The post-1870 period produced new forms of industrial capital centered on scale, machinery, infrastructure, and immobile assets.
- Large industrial firms displaced or subordinated some older merchant forms of capital.
- National states became more central to capital accumulation through tariffs, law, military power, infrastructure, and imperial rivalry.
- Despite nationalist self-understandings, industrial capital remained globally dependent on markets, raw materials, finance, and labor.

## Evidence Anchors

- Opens with Carl Röchling and large-scale iron and steel production as a sign of capital's new scale.
- Links firm consolidation to state power and global competition.
- Ends by emphasizing capitalism's need for more land, raw materials, and workers.
