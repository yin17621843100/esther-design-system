# Capitalism: A Global History - Chapter 3: The Great Connecting, 1450-1650

## Summary

Chapter 3 describes the early modern intensification of global connections. Beckert uses Surat and other trade centers to show how European expansion entered existing Asian, African, and Indian Ocean networks, creating a more connected and hierarchical archipelago of capital.

## Key Claims

- The period from 1450 to 1650 did not create global trade from nothing; it intensified and reconfigured older networks.
- European merchants initially depended on established non-European commercial systems and intermediaries.
- The new connectedness increased diversity in institutions, labor regimes, and forms of rule, but also sharpened hierarchy.
- Expanding merchant capital began to break into production on a larger scale, especially through mines, plantations, and colonial extraction.

## Evidence Anchors

- Surat appears as a major trading hub where Gujarati, Persian, Ottoman, Armenian, European, and Arab actors interacted.
- European trading companies are shown as latecomers that had to adapt to powerful existing networks.
- The chapter closes by presenting connected diversity as both a source of capitalist flexibility and a basis for hierarchy.
