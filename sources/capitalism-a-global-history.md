# Capitalism: A Global History

## Bibliographic Details

- **Author:** [[sven-beckert]]
- **Publisher:** Penguin Publishing Group
- **Publication date:** 2025-11-25
- **ISBN:** 9780735220843
- **Language:** English
- **Raw source:** `raw/Capitalism+A+Global+History+by+Sven+Beckert.epub`

## Summary

Sven Beckert presents capitalism as a world-historical force that reshaped labor, production, markets, politics, landscapes, consumption, and human self-understanding. The book frames capitalism not as a purely European or national story, but as a global process that emerged from long-distance trade, imperial expansion, coerced labor, industrial transformation, state-building, rebellion, postwar stabilization, and neoliberal restructuring.

The book's central pattern is developmental and global: early "islands of capital" and merchant networks became more powerful through global connection, countryside transformation, intensified manufacturing, plantation systems, and state-backed expansion. Industrial capitalism then reorganized production and labor on a much larger scale. Later crises and rebellions forced reconstructions of capital, labor, and state power. The twentieth century brought industrial capitalism's violent breakdowns, postcolonial challenges, post-1945 taming, and the neoliberal age after 1973.

## Key Claims

- [[capitalism]] is treated as a process rather than a single event or invention.
- Capitalism was "born global" in the sense that its history depends on cross-regional trade, imperial connections, commodity frontiers, and globally distributed labor regimes.
- The capitalist revolution depended on markets and capital accumulation, but also on political power, coercion, slavery, colonialism, and state action.
- [[industrial-capitalism]] was not capitalism's origin, but a major acceleration that transformed manufacturing, labor, and global commodity flows.
- Capitalism repeatedly faced resistance from enslaved people, workers, peasants, anti-colonial movements, and states seeking to redirect or contain it.
- The post-1945 order temporarily "tamed" industrial capitalism in many industrial heartlands through higher growth, reduced inequality, welfare states, and mass consumption.
- The [[neoliberal-age]] after 1973 restored market-oriented policies under conditions that often weakened labor and increased inequality.

## Chapter Map

### Preface

[[capitalism-a-global-history-preface]] frames capitalism as the force that shapes modern work, consumption, politics, cities, landscapes, and global interdependence.

### Introduction

[[capitalism-a-global-history-introduction]] uses the 1639 Robert Keayne case in Massachusetts to show that profit-seeking norms were historically contested rather than natural.

### Chapter 1: Islands of Capital

[[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-01-islands-of-capital]] argues that capitalism has no single origin point. It emerged as a process through scattered nodes of capital, trade, finance, and commodity exchange.

### Chapter 2: Capitalists Without Capitalism

[[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-02-capitalists-without-capitalism]] describes premodern and early merchant networks across Asia, Africa, Europe, and the Americas as important but still thin "rivulets" of capital.

### Part I: Building Capitalism

- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-03-the-great-connecting]] covers the global connecting of 1450-1650.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-04-transforming-the-countryside]] follows capital into plantations, rural production, land, labor, and imperial commodity systems.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-05-intensifying-industry]] explains preindustrial manufacturing intensification and rural production.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-06-the-perfect-storm]] describes the convergence that prepared the way for industrial capitalism.

### Part II: The Great Leap

- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-07-the-rise-of-industrial-capitalism]] treats the Industrial Revolution as capitalism's major acceleration.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-08-capturing-the-hinterland]] links industrial centers to commodity frontiers and coercive labor.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-09-a-capitalist-civilization]] shows capitalism becoming a broader material and cultural order.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-10-rebellions]] presents rebellion as a force that cracked old-regime capitalism.

### Part III: Global Reconstructions

- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-11-reconstructing-capital]] covers large firms, heavy industry, fixed investment, and reorganized capital.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-12-reconstructing-labor]] examines new systems for recruiting, moving, disciplining, and retaining labor.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-13-enclosures]] broadens enclosure to states, territories, law, empire, and economic categories.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-14-a-time-of-monsters]] treats 1918-1945 as a crisis of industrial capitalism.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-15-insurgents]] covers postcolonial industrial sovereignty and development.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-16-taming-industrial-capitalism]] describes the post-1945 taming of industrial capitalism.

### Part IV: The Future of Capitalism?

- [[capitalism-a-global-history-chapter-17-riding-the-tiger]] examines the neoliberal age from 1973-2008.
- [[capitalism-a-global-history-epilogue]] returns to the future of capitalism through Cambodian garment workers and capital's territorial escape routes.
